The Rock Cycle

What is the Rock Cycle?

The rock cycle is the physical and chemical process by which rocks, magma and sediments change form. It involves the following processes and properties, starting and ending with magma

  • Cooling and Hardening
  • Weathering and Erosion
  • Compaction and Cementation
  • Heating and Pressuring
  • Melting


Igneous Rocks

Igneous rocks are formed from cooled magma, either slowly deep inside the Earth, or quickly as cooled lava from volcanoes. These different cooling processes (fast and slow) create different types of igneous rocks — fast cooling creates pebbled rocks with visible particles and holes, such as granite, whereas slow cooling creates smooth rocks such as norite.

  • Igneous rocks - formed by cooling
  • Fast cooling - pebbled and holey rocks
  • Slow cooling - smooth and hard rocks
  • Granite and Norite


Sedimentary Rocks

Sediments are formed when igneous rocks are weathered and eroded by physical processes such as water, wind, or ice. These sediments are then deposited, compacted and cemented to form sedimentary rocks, which often contain layers of different sediments, as well as organic material such as fossils. Sedimentary rocks include sandstone and limestone.

  • Compacting and Cementation
  • Sandstone and Limestone


Metamorphic Rocks

Metamorphic rocks are formed from either igneous or sedimentary rocks. These rocks are placed under extreme pressure and heat, often deep inside the Earth, and they become extremely hard and smooth, and sometimes contain wave-like patterns and impressions, like marble.

  • Heat and Pressure
  • Marble


Magma

Magma is the substance from which all rocks are formed, and it is found deep below the crust, in the region of the Earth known as the mantle, which is about 15-25 km below the surface of the Earth.

  • Deep below the surface of the Earth
  • Forms all rocks
